Analysis of the association between working hours per week and GDP per capita

 

This report summarizes the analysis of the association between working hours per week and GDP per capita. Figure 1 displays the average working hours per week across countries in 2017. Countries with longer working hours are shown in darker blue, while those with shorter working hours are in lighter blue. It can be observed that many developing countries tend to have longer working hours.

 

Figure 1. Working hours per week, 2017

 

Figure 2 shows the global distribution of GDP per capita in 2017. Countries with higher GDP per capita are shown in darker green, while those with lower values are in lighter green. We can see significant variation across regions, with North America, Western Europe, Australia, and parts of East Asia showing higher GDP per capita. Much of Africa and parts of Asia show lower GDP per capita. The highest GDP per capita values appear to be concentrated in small, wealthy nations and oil-rich states

 

Figure 2. GDP per capita (price and inflation adjusted, in PPP$2017), 2017

 

The scatterplot in Figure 3 reveals an interesting negative relationship between working hours and GDP per capita. Countries with longer working hours tend to have lower GDP per capita, while countries with shorter working hours generally have higher GDP per capita.

 

Figure 3. Relationship between working hours per week and GDP per capita, 2017

 

The analysis reveals a statistically significant negative correlation (r = -0.24, p = 0.013) between working hours and GDP per capita. On average, each working hour of work per week is associated with a decrease of appropriately 1,447.2 international dollars in GDP per capita.

 

This negative relationship might seem counterintuitive at first, but it aligns with economic theories suggesting that more productive economies (with higher GDP per capita) can afford shorter working hours while maintaining high output. More developed economies often have higher labor productivity, better technology, and stronger institutions that enable workers to produce more value in less time.

 

The R-squared value of 0.059 indicates that working hours explain about 5.9% of the variation in GDP per capita, suggesting that while there is a significant relationship, many other factors also influence a country's economic output per person such as historical factors, infrastructure development, investment rates, and many other economic and social factors.
